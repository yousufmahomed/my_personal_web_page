From Paper to Production

Solving the "Night Call" Communication Gap through Agile Web Engineering.

The "Night Call" Challenge

In our district, referring hospitals struggled with a critical communication gap: **identifying the specific doctor on night call.** Initial solutions considered included Google Sheets and scanned PDF documents, but these lacked the real-time accessibility, security, and version control required for high-stakes medical staffing.

I engineered this custom web application in parallel to these ideas to demonstrate that a modern, cloud-first approach was the only way to ensure a "single source of truth" for clinical coordination.

The Agile Lifecycle

The development followed a rigorous engineering cycle, prioritizing stakeholder input and iterative refactoring to meet the needs of front-line medical staff.

  • Stakeholder Collaboration: Requirements gathering from district HODs and medical management.
  • Version Control: Managed various logic branches via **GitHub**, allowing for seamless refactoring during beta testing.
  • Beta Testing: Conducted user testing with clinical staff to refine the UI/UX for mobile accessibility.

[Image: GitHub Network Graph or Commit History showing active refactoring]

Institutional Resilience

While the technical solution was well-received by medical leadership, the primary obstacle was navigating a slow-moving government bureaucracy. Despite the technical success and readiness of the platform, administrative hurdles prevented official district-wide adoption.

"This project stands as a high-fidelity proof-of-concept, proving that clinical leadership can successfully architect enterprise-grade solutions even when faced with institutional inertia."

Phase 2: The Notion ED Engine

Building on the architectural logic of the web project, I developed a highly complex **Electronic Emergency Department Roster** using Notion. This system manages the granular requirements of internal departmental staffing and serves as a core management tool in my current HOD role.
